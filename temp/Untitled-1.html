Basic Definitions

I have always believed that engineering is the process of designing/implementing solutions to any problem facing a person or person's. The dictionary defines Engineering as " the application of science and mathematics by which the properties of matter and the sources of energy in nature are made useful to people" [1]. Another, perhaps more applicable definition, with its emphasis on 'design, planning, and implementation based on scientific principle' is the Wikipedia intro blurb for the Engineering article :

"Engineering is the use of scientific principles to design and build machines, structures, and other items, including bridges, tunnels, roads, vehicles, and buildings. [(3)] The discipline of engineering encompasses a broad range of more specialized fields of engineering, each with a more specific emphasis on particular areas of applied mathematics, applied science, and types of application. "[2]

This definition borrows from two sources, in the former sentence we have another dictionary definition, this time the Cambridge English Dictionary [3],[4], and the "About" page for the IAENG (International Association of Engineers) website for the latter. Interestingly, the Merriam-Webster definition seems lifted from this site's definition, or vice-versa.

Discussion on the Above and Exposition of Ideals
The General Case

Based on these descriptions, that engineering is the application of science or mathematics to solve a (generally) well-defined and circumscribed problem. The term itself is inexact, as there are many different disciplines of "Engineering".

Engineering is the process by which engineers research, analyze, develop, test, modify and maintain a certain type of product. Regardless of the type of engineering, whether mechanical, software, chemical, environmental and etc. the process always involves designing and constructing a final product. (abscription?).

It may be first posited that generally, science is the exploration of the natural world, and its precise description, classification, enumeration using subject appropriate terms. In addition, this science is also the elucidation of the terra incognita in a given field. In the general sense, the term '<x> science', often refers to, in general meaning, the "pure" science. To know for the sake of knowing, for knowing itself has value. As declared by Dutch theologian, Cornelius Jansen (1585–1638), 'a desire for knowledge was one form of concupiscence, and he argued that from this “illness … arises the investigation of nature's secrets (which are irrelevant to us), knowledge of which is useless, and which men do not wish to know except for the sake of knowing them”.'
